The "Weird but Exciting" Life in India

An NRI's honest field notes from Kerala holidays. Hartals, random relatives, wedding feasts, and the fine art of getting embarrassed in situations I never saw coming.

Nakshatra RP · Jun 4, 2021 · 2 min read

I must admit that understanding the day-to-day life in India is harder than eating my veggies (Actually not- eating veggies is still on top of my "hard-to-do" list!) but one thing I know for sure is that, an NRI like me would need a 10-volume survival guide before planning to get settled in India.

I must have gone for a vacation in Kerala for more than a dozen times, and each time I go, I learn the art of getting randomly embarrassed at absolutely unfamiliar situations. I recollect the time when I stayed at my grandparents' house and someone visited us to inform that the next day is Hartal. "Oh noo...", said everyone in unison and I was the one sitting there in front of my Stuart Little cartoon while my 2nd-grader-brain was still trying to understand what a "Hartal" is. Unexpectedly (for me), the next day seemed to be more pleasant than usual; no annoying vehicles or relatives trespassing at odd times. I observed these and made a mental note that Hartals are god-given holidays to take a day off from socializing. All thanks to Mahatma Gandhi who gave us this idea.

There's another conclusion that I have drawn from my vacation experiences in Kerala: It's not advisable to sit somewhere doing nothing in your own house because you can't predict when a random aunty or uncle who you have never met in your life walks into your home to make you rethink your life's decisions. As a student, I spend my time very wisely working hard throughout my academic term (Not bragging, of course!) but summer vacation is the time when I switch to my sloth-personality. That explains a lot why my relatives see me as a person who sleeps for half of the day and spends my remaining time eating and watching TV.

Now talking about marriages and reception ceremonies, meeting our cousin-gang is the best thing that happens during these. And while we have the time of our lives, there are older relatives taking their position to question us about our memory of their existence ("Do you know me dear? You were a small baby when I first saw you!"). The biggest concern during those events was whether we would be able to catch a place in the first round of guests to have the feast. It is also important to show off your table manners when the videographer comes to shoot the banquet. Perhaps being a permanent resident of India is better than going for holidays but who could possibly disagree that life in India is unpredictable but AWESOME!
