Comparison: When isn't it relevant?

You cannot ask a banana to have the fibre content of an apple. So why do we keep asking children to be someone else's version of success? A case against comparison culture.

Nakshatra RP · Apr 5, 2021 · 2 min read

Comparison is advantageous when it comes to determining development of various countries or selecting participants for a specific competition. It is also helpful when choosing your representatives in an election. But the "compare and contrast" strategy used by parents to their children and teachers to students is the most useless form of comparison I've seen.

Imagine there's an apple and a banana in front you. Both these fruits are very different in their appearances as well as qualities. You can't demand a banana to have as much of fibre as that of an apple. Similarly, you can't complain that the apple doesn't have high levels of potassium like the banana. Thus, you choose your fruits according to your needs. But what is being done by parents these days is that they expect their children to be good at everything. They also want their child to walk on footprints of other great people, not realizing how valuable their own identity and footprints are.

If Albert Einstein had been forced by his parents to study a popular degree and get a high-paid job, he would've never been one of the greatest physicists of all time. He had passion in what he studied and it led him to make some of the most important contributions in the field of Science. It would have been absurd if someone had compared him with the great Indian mathematician, Srinivasa Ramanujan. Both of them have excelled in their respective fields and no one has ever compared them (at least, not after their achievements). No one has asked about their knowledge in other subjects either.

But what if the world had only Physics and Math geniuses? Would it have been enough to know the mysteries of the universe and the deepest sciences without social order? And here comes the role of knowing our history and the working of institutions. Social science isn't something that anyone can mug up and be good at. It takes immense interest to have the determination to study about the society and put it to practical use. Social sciences concern people's relationships and interactions with one another. Without it, the world would have been in a turmoil.

Every subject has it's own importance and one is never superior to another. Indian parents are known to compare their children to take up what others have been successful in and this has led to a high percentage of population being unhappy with their career and unable to make distinguished contributions in their field. I still hear parents comparing their children with their peers who get straight A's. Society judges those who have followed unconventional career paths. Therefore, It's nugatory to compare the abilities of students in different subjects.

Let go of the stereotypes and support our youngsters to fly high!
