Ignorance is not bliss, it's Fault

We teach girls to protect themselves from men, but rarely teach boys to be better. On why the missing piece in the conversation about women's safety starts at home.

Nakshatra RP · Aug 4, 2021 · 3 min read

Another day. Another crime against women. Women's safety still remains a great concern in the present generation despite feminist efforts to eradicate gender-related homicides. According to the UNODC, a total of 87,000 women were intentionally killed in 2017. More than half of them (58 per cent) - 50,000 were killed by intimate partners or family members, meaning that 137 women across the world are killed by a member of their own family every day. More than a third (30,000) of the women intentionally killed in 2017 were killed by their current or former intimate partner - someone they would normally expect to trust.

Although countries have taken action to address violence against women by adopting legal changes, something seems to be missing. It is nothing but the social efforts in guiding males that seem to be missing. We have done all that we could to restrict females in order to protect them, from teaching them defense techniques to warning them about red flags. But these efforts are being futile since no advancements are made to bring awareness of such issues to the other gender. The deep-rooted patriarchy has influenced the minds of everyone in the society that sons are given more freedom than daughters. Parents don't realize that if they have to protect their daughters, they have to play their role in teaching their sons to respect women. The society's ignorance towards the male gender, except for blaming them, is faulty.

The root of violence against women are gender inequalities. The first step in tackling this issue starts at home. If you're a parent, here's what you can do:

  • Speak up: If you notice your boy being disrespectful to his sister or a female friend, tell him that it's wrong to be so. Teach him that masculinity is all about respecting boundaries. When he's old enough, let him know about gender equality and how to attain it.
  • Be an Example: The best way to teach your children to break the social norms is to be their role models. Treat your children equally regardless of their gender. Show them that both parents have equal power in the household. Don't express violence and disrespect towards your spouse.
  • Hear them out: Give values to their emotions and allow them to express themselves freely so that they don't have to take out their frustration in an unhealthy manner. They might experience dilemmas throughout their stages of growth. Help them deal with it.
  • Spread Awareness: Teach them that a woman should never be objectified. Stop normalizing certain behaviours in boys. Let them know that pornography and sexual violence are not real. Teach them that women don't exist to be violently and sexually disrespected. Avoid media that promotes stereotypes.
  • Fight stereotypes: Stop judging children on the basis of their gender. Avoid making sexist statements such as "You play like a girl", "Boys don't cry", "Girls mature early so learn to compromise". The constant use of these comments on children can imprint stereotypical thoughts and influence their behaviours in adulthood. It may compel them to have the need to control women.
  • Encourage others: Remember that the change starts with you. Create the ripple effect. Don't be afraid to correct others when they say the wrong thing about genders. Express yourselves in social media and other platforms in order to educate others.

Always remember that feminism is not synonymous with misandry (or man-hating). Feminism is rather a solution to promote gender equality while empowering women as well as encouraging men to be better individuals to create a safe environment for everyone around them. Break the silence about gender-related issues and be the change you want to see!
